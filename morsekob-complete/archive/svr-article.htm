Everything old is new: Area inventor taps Morse code

By Robert Baldino
Contributing Writer


Les Kerr of Snoqualmie invented software
to use Morse code on the Internet

Like most young boys of a certain generation, Les Kerr grew up loving trains and the railroad. He was mesmerized by the big machines, the sounds and the power, but also by the men in the railway stations he saw tapping away on strange little devices called telegraphs.

“I would hang around the local railroad depot and I would hear this kind of clickity-clack stuff and it just fascinated me,” said Kerr.

Kerr wasn’t so much fascinated by the telegraph itself as he was the mysterious language being transmitted across the copper wires. In his teenage years that fascination led him to the hobby of ham radio operation, and the language of radio Morse code, better known as International Morse.

The mystery was only half solved at that point, though, for International Morse is slightly different than the older code once used by the railroads and Western Union, known as American Morse.

“I was very proficient at the radio Morse code, but [American Morse] was still a mystery to me,” said Kerr.

American Morse would remain a mystery to Kerr for decades until he found himself volunteering at the Northwest Railway Museum in Snoqualmie in 2002. There he met James Sackey, who told him about a computer program for learning American Morse. Kerr, a retired software engineer, was instantly intrigued.

Kerr found the program and put his knack for learning languages to work. “With the help of that program, that’s how I learned American Morse,” said Kerr. “And then I wanted to use it. It’s not much fun knowing a code if you have no one to talk to.”

Kerr set about trying to find telegraphy enthusiasts like himself, and the Internet proved to be a great resource. He quickly learned of Morse activity over a dial-up hub.

Those early dial-up experiences were fun for Kerr, for they introduced him to like-minded hobbyists and took him back in time. “They would get together over the phone and chat away,” said Kerr. “And I really wanted to hear that because I wanted to kind of go back to my childhood.”

Kerr’s amusement soon gave way to frustration. The dial-up scenario required obsolete modems and some tinkering with the computer, and it could get quite expensive using long-distance phone lines. He figured there had to be a better way and put his computing skills to the test.

Kerr began developing a software program that would allow him to send Morse code over the Internet – similar to what some people use for long-distance phone service – rather than over phone lines. Despite his background with software engineering, it was quite an undertaking.

“It was a huge challenge because it was a kind of programming I had never done before,” said Kerr, who had no experience with Windows-based programming. “I just had a lot to learn and no one to work with, so that was part of the challenge – doing it on my own.”

The process took two years, and Kerr’s software went through several generations before he finally hit upon a satisfactory iteration, the Morse KOB, for Morse Key-On-Board, the literal telegraph key and sounder on a board of wood that we’re all so familiar with thanks to old movies. Kerr’s Morse KOB program was released July 27, 2005.

Using the program and a few bits and pieces from the local electronics store, a user can rig a telegraph up to their computer and send Morse code anywhere in the world via the Internet.

The program can also translate Morse code signals into English text (although not quite with 100 percent accuracy) and can translate words typed on the keyboard into Morse and send them out using the KOB program.

The KOB itself isn’t even required, since the program allows a user to send and receive Morse code using the computer’s keyboard and speakers, if necessary.

The Morse KOB program has been a revelation for enthusiasts all over the world, especially since the dial-up hub would soon no longer be an option.

“They said, ‘This is what we wanted all the time,’” said Kerr.

Some 200 people in more than 20 countries have downloaded the program, and there’s a small core group who are quite active online, many of them retired telegraph operators who are happy to have a chance to practice their art once again, which is exciting for Kerr.

“Not too many people under the age of 65 have ever heard Morse code in real action,” said Kerr.

On this day, Kerr is carrying on a conversation with three men in various parts of the world. There’s Dave in Missouri, an 80-year-old former railroad telegrapher; there’s 72-year-old Keith in Illinois, another retired railroad man; and in far away New Zealand there’s 74-year-old Maurie, who used to code telegrams for the post office.

It’s bittersweet for Kerr, knowing that as this older generation is fading away, his beloved American Morse will likely fade away with them.

While watching Kerr click out messages, one can’t help but be struck by how anachronistic the whole setup is. One also realizes that the computer connection is a vital anachronism for those who practice the art – a way to not forget, nor be forgotten.

“I’m hoping that this provides a tool, like a spring board, that will allow people who have just a glimmer of an interest to make it easier for them to get in and allow it to grow if that’s what they want to do,” Kerr said.

Kerr isn’t quite done with the program yet, either. He’d like to add some training features, and improve its general operation.

“It’s a lifetime project,” Kerr said.

To learn more about Les Kerr’s Morse KOB program and telegraphy, visit www.morsekob.org.
